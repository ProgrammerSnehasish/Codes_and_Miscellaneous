<p><u><font color="red" size="6" face="STXingkai">Introduction:</font></u></p>

<p><img src="https://www.history.com/.image/t_share/MTgwOTQyNDE3MDk5MzAyMjQ4/eid-al-fitr-gettyimages-1148084709.jpg" height="120" widht="200" broder="2" align="right"/><font color="black" size="3" face="Stliti"><b><a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eid_al-Fitr"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli"><u>Eid al-Fitr</u></font></a> was originated by the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli"><u>Islamic prophet Muhammad</u></font></a>. According to certain traditions, these festivals were initiated in <font color="green" face="MV Boli">Medina</font> after the <font color="green" face="MV Boli">migration of Muhammad</font> from <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mecca"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli"><u>Mecca</u></font></a>. <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anas_ibn_Malik"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli"><u>Anas</u></font></a>, a well-known companion of the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prophets_and_messengers_in_Islam"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli"><u>Islamic prophet</u></font></a>, narrated that, when <font color="green" face="MV Boli">Muhammad</font> arrived in <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medina"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli"><u>Medina</u></font></a>, he found people celebrating two specific days in which they entertain themselves with recreation and merriment. At this, <font color="green" face="MV Boli">Muhammad</font> remarked that <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allah"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli"><u>Allah</u></font></a> had fixed two days of festivity: <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eid_al-Fitr"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli"><u>Eid al-Fitr</u></font></a> and <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eid_al-Adha"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli"><u>Eid al-Adha</u></font></a>.</b></font></p>

<p><font color="black" size="3" face="Stliti"><b><font color="green" face="MV Boli">Eid al-Fitr</font> which literally means 'Feast of Breaking the Fast', is a <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_holidays"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli"><u>Islamic festival</u></font></a> celebrated by <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muslims"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli"><u>Muslim</u></font></a> worldwide as  it marks the end of the month-long dawn-to-sunset fasting of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramadan"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli"><u>Ramadan</u></font></a>. It falls on the first day of <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shawwal#:~:text=Shawwal%20(Arabic%3A%20%D8%B4%D9%8E%D9%88%D9%8E%D9%91%D8%A7%D9%84%E2%80%8E%2C,at%20this%20time%20of%20year."><font color="blue" face="MV Boli"><u>Shawwal in the Islamic Calendar</u></font></a>.</b></font></p>

<p><u><font color="red" size="6" face="STXingkai">Rituals & Celebration:</font></u></p>

<p><img src="https://sabrangindia.in/sites/default/files/hindumuslim-1-1472706220.jpg?237" height="100" width="200" align="left"/><font color="black" size="3" face="Stliti"><b>Traditionally, <font color="green" face="MV Boli">Eid al-Fitr</font> begins at sunset on the night of the first sighting of the crescent moon. If the moon is not observed immediately after the 29th day of the previous <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_month"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli">lunar month</font></a> (either because clouds block its view or because the western sky is still too bright when the moon sets), then the holiday is celebrated the following day. <font color="green" face="MV Boli">Eid al-Fitr</font> is celebrated for one to three days, depending on the country. It is forbidden to fast on the Day of Eid, and a specific prayer is nominated for this day. As an obligatory act of charity, money is paid to the poor and the needy (<font color="green" face="MV Boli">Zakat-ul-fitr</font>) before performing the <font color="green" face="MV Boli">‘Eid prayer’</font>.<img src="https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a0/Eid_al-Fitr_prayer%2C_Suleymaniye_Mosque%2C_Istanbul_-_Aug_30%2C_2011.jpg" height="120" width="200" align="right"/> <font color="green" face="MV Boli">Eid al-Fitr</font> has a particular <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salah"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli">salat (Islamic prayer)</font></a> that consists of two <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rakat"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli">rakats</font></a> (units) generally performed in an open field or large hall. It may only be performed in congregation (<font color="green" face="MV Boli">jamāʿat</font>) and features six additional <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Takbir"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli">Takbirs</font></a> (raising of the hands to the ears while saying <font color="green" face="MV Boli">"Allāhu ʾAkbar"</font>, meaning <font color="green" face="MV Boli">"God is the greatest"</font>) in the <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanafi"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli">Hanafi school of Sunni Islam</font></a>: three at the start of the <font color="green" face="MV Boli">first rakat</font> and three just before <font color="green" face="">rukūʿ</font> in the <font color="green" face="MV Boli">second rakat</font>. Other <font color="green" face="MV Boli">Sunni schools</font> usually have <font color="green" face="MV Boli">12 Takbirs</font>, similarly split in groups of seven and five. In <a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shia_Islam"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli">Shia Islam</font></a>, the <font color="green" face="MV Boli">salat</font> has <font color="green" face="MV Boli">six Takbirs</font> in the <font color="green" face="MV Boli">first rakat</font> at the <font color="green" face="MV Boli">end of qira'a, before rukūʿ</font>, and five in the second. Depending on the <font color="green" face="MV Boli">juristic opinion</font> of the locality, this salat is either <font color="green" face="MV Boli">farḍ (obligatory), mustaḥabb (strongly recommended) or mandūb ( preferable)</font>. After the <font color="green" face="MV Boli">salat</font>, <font color="green" face="MV Boli">Muslims</font> celebrate the <font color="green" face="MV Boli">Eid al-Fitr</font> in various ways with food ("<a href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eid_cuisine"><font color="blue" face="MV Boli">Eid cuisine</font></a>") being a central theme, which also gives the holiday the nickname <font color="green" face="MV Boli">"Sweet Eid" or "Sugar Feast"</font>.</b></font></p>
